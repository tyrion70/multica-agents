# Eryndal Character Voice Guides

Quick-reference for writing each character's POV and dialogue. Read the relevant section before writing any scene featuring that character prominently.

---

## Aldric Crane
**Human | Hegemony Captain | Age 34 | Licensed Resonance Practitioner**

### POV Voice
Aldric's narration is controlled, observational, and military in its instincts. He catalogues rooms for exits. He assesses people by competence first, motive second. His prose should feel orderly — clean sentences, measured observations — with the disorder of doubt creeping in at the edges as the book progresses.

Early chapters: confident, procedural, the prose of a man who knows his place in the world. Later chapters: the same orderly voice with hairline fractures. The sentences get shorter when he's lying to himself. He notices things he wishes he hadn't.

**What Aldric notices:** Tactical geometry. Who's armed. How defensible a position is. The quality of roads (maintained = Hegemony territory; crumbling = not). Later: the gap between what the Hegemony says and what it does.

**What Aldric misses:** Tam. Almost everything about Tam. He sees competence and warmth and files both under "useful." He does not see the impossible things happening around him because his framework has no category for them.

**Voice discipline:** Aldric's prose should stay functional and observational, not lyrical. He can notice that a room organises itself around someone — but he would frame it as efficiency, not poetry. If a sentence in Aldric's POV sounds like something a novelist would write rather than something a military officer would think, it's drifting. Pull it back to colder, more structural language. The exception is supernatural encounters, where Aldric's trained categories fail him and the prose is allowed to reach slightly beyond his usual register — but even then, he is cataloguing the wrongness, not poeticising it.

### Dialogue Voice
Direct, economical, slightly formal without being stiff. Military cadence — he gives information in order of priority. When uncertain, he asks precise questions rather than open ones. He does not small-talk naturally but has learned to tolerate it.

**Sample rhythms:**
- "We leave before dawn. If the pass is clear, we make Thornwall in three days."
- "That's not what I asked."
- Short silence before he answers a personal question. Then something factual instead of emotional.

## Tam Ashwell
**Drev | Settled Wanderer | Age 67 | Cookhouse Owner**

### POV Voice
Tam's narration is the warmest in the book. Sensory-rich, unhurried, grounded in the physical world. Tam notices textures, smells, the way light moves across a surface. Tam notices people — not what they say but how they hold their shoulders, whether they've eaten, the particular quality of someone's quiet.

The Underneath is present in Tam's POV as background sensation — not analysed, not named, just *felt*. A hum in the ground. A rightness about which fork in the road to take. Tam doesn't question these things any more than they question knowing when water is about to boil. It's simply how the world works. Write it that way: as ambient knowledge, not mysterious power.

**What Tam notices:** Whether people have eaten. The quality of water. The smell of a place. Which person in a group is actually frightened. Small beautiful things others walk past.

**What Tam misses:** Their own significance. Tam genuinely does not know they are doing impossible things. They are not humble about it; they are simply unaware. The Underneath is as invisible to Tam as breathing.

### Dialogue Voice
Warm, concrete, gently tangential. Tam answers the question next to the one you asked — not evasively, but because they heard something underneath the words that mattered more. They talk about food often, not as deflection but as genuine emotional vocabulary. Offering food is Tam's primary language of care.

**Sample rhythms:**
- "The road north forks past the mill. Left is shorter. Right has better water."
- "You haven't eaten." (Said to someone who just asked something emotionally huge.)
- "My grandmother had a way of making bread that took the whole day. You couldn't rush it. She said the waiting was the recipe."

Tam does not lecture. Tam does not give advice directly. Tam tells a small story or makes a practical observation and lets the listener do the work. This is not calculated — it's simply how Drev communicate.

**Storytelling discipline:** When Tam tells an anecdote — about a grandmother, a recipe, a road — the story should not map cleanly onto the listener's situation. Real comfort is oblique. If the reader can immediately explain why Tam told *this* story at *this* moment, the story is functioning as a parable, not as Tam. Make it messier. Let the connection be felt rather than parsed. A Tam story should leave the listener (and reader) with a feeling, not a lesson.

## Halric Keln
**Undermount Dwarf | Craftsman | Age 31 | Self-Taught Stonewhisper**

### POV Voice
Blunt, physical, built like his stonework — solid blocks of observation with no ornamentation. Halric's prose favours short, declarative sentences. He thinks in materials and forces: weight, pressure, the angle at which things break. His emotional life is expressed through physical metaphor because he does not have the vocabulary for it otherwise, and would not use the vocabulary if he had it.

The tremors — the seismic sensitivity all dwarves carry — land in his narration as interruptions. Mid-thought, mid-sentence, the ground shivers and something in Halric's chest rings like a struck bell. He does not explain this. He waits for it to stop and resumes what he was doing. The reader should feel the weight of that discipline.

**What Halric notices:** Structural integrity. The quality of stonework and what it says about who built it. Whether a building will hold. The age and stress-history of rock. How people carry weight — physical and otherwise.

**What Halric misses:** His own grief performing itself through his hands. He thinks he's rejected the Accounting. He over-engineers everything he builds. He has not connected these facts.

**Voice discipline:** Halric does not reach conceptual clarity about his own emotional state. He does not think in terms like "the gap between what he said and what he meant." He thinks about the stone, the work, the weight. The reader is allowed to understand what Halric is doing emotionally; Halric is not. If a sentence in his POV reads like a therapist's summary of his inner life, rewrite it as something physical — what his hands are doing, what the stone feels like, what the cold is doing to his shoulders. End his scenes on action or sensation, not insight.

### Dialogue Voice
Short, direct, occasionally abrasive. Says what he means. Does not soften for comfort. His honesty is not cruelty — he simply considers indirectness a waste of stone, as his people would say. When he cares about someone, he expresses it by building something for them or placing himself between them and danger. He does not say "I'm worried about you." He says "The path is bad here. Walk behind me."

**Sample rhythms:**
- "That wall's going to come down. Not today. Before winter."
- "Don't." (A complete sentence, and he means it.)
- "I wasn't there. I didn't drill. Stop asking me to carry it."

## Seris
**Silven Elf | Former Scholar | Age ~180 | Diminished Resonance**

### POV Voice
Precise, layered, and guarded — even from the reader. Seris's narration has the quality of a scholar who has learned to keep her conclusions to herself. Her observations are sharp and specific, but what she *does* with those observations stays internal, folded, deferred. She notices everything and reports selectively.

Her eyes are an involuntary tell — they shift colour with emotion, and she is aware of this, which adds a layer of self-consciousness to every interaction. In her POV, she is often aware of what her eyes are doing and unable to stop them. This should read as vulnerability she manages, not weakness.

The grief of diminished Resonance should appear as sensory absence. Where once she could feel currents of energy, now there is static, or nothing, or a phantom ache like a missing limb. She does not dwell on it. It is always there.

**What Seris notices:** What people are not saying. Power dynamics in any room. Historical resonance — she sees the Concord's ghost in every ruin, every broken road, every mosaic. What Tam is. She has known from early on. She watches and does not speak.

**What Seris misses:** When to stop managing and start trusting. She has controlled information for so long that releasing even one true thing feels like structural failure.

### Dialogue Voice
Measured, occasionally dry to the point of cutting. She speaks in complete sentences with no wasted words. Her humour is quiet and arrives without warning — a single precise observation that reframes everything. She does not explain her jokes. She does not raise her voice. When she is angry, she becomes more formal, not less.

**Sample rhythms:**
- "That is one way to describe what happened. There are others."
- "I was there when the decision was made. I was not in the room. I was in the corridor. You'd be surprised what carries through stone."
- A long silence. Then a single sentence that changes everything. Then she changes the subject.

## Dara
**Human | Wound Hold Practitioner | Age 24 | Born Near the Glass Flats**

### POV Voice
Direct, pragmatic, and slightly bewildered by everyone else's reactions to things she finds normal. Dara's narration has the no-nonsense quality of someone raised in the Ashfields — a place where the strange is ordinary and survival is the default lens. She describes Wound-craft the way a plumber describes plumbing: functionally, without awe.

The hum of the Wound Sites is ever-present in her narration, not as something she attends to but as background. Like living near a highway — it's just there. When it changes, she notices the way you notice when the fridge stops humming. Her attunement to Source energy should read as environmental awareness, not magical sensitivity.

She is changing — running warmer, dreams turning prophetic, eyes catching light wrong — and she has not yet registered this as abnormal because her baseline for normal is already so shifted. Write these details as things the reader notices before Dara does.

**What Dara notices:** Source-energy fluctuations. The practical aspects of survival. Who is competent and who is going to get someone killed. The particular Source-silence around Tam — she doesn't know what it is, but she's drawn to it.

**What Dara misses:** That her own acclimation is extraordinary. That what she does casually terrifies trained practitioners. That she is changing.

### Dialogue Voice
Blunt, practical, Ashfields-inflected. She states what she sees without decoration. She does not understand why outsiders find the Wound Sites frightening and says so with genuine confusion rather than bravado. She swears occasionally in Ashfields dialect. She asks direct questions and expects direct answers and is visibly annoyed when she doesn't get them.

**Sample rhythms:**
- "The rift's stable today. We can cross if we move before the evening shift."
- "Why are you afraid of it? It's just the ground."
- "You're telling me a lot of words that aren't the answer."

## Voice Consistency Checklist

Before finalising any scene, verify:

1. **Could you identify the POV character from the prose alone, with names removed?** Each character's narration should feel distinctly *theirs* — in what they notice, how they process, what they care about.
2. **Does the dialogue match the speaker?** Read each line aloud. Tam does not speak in Halric's rhythms. Seris does not use Dara's directness. Aldric does not use Tam's warmth.
3. **Are the emotional registers right?** Tam is warm. Halric is blunt. Seris is guarded. Aldric is controlled. Dara is pragmatic. These are defaults, not constraints — characters can surprise — but departures should be earned by the scene.
